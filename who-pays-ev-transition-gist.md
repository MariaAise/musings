# Who Pays for the EV Transition? — Article Gist for Co-Author Review

**Working headline:** *Who Pays for the EV Transition? Inside the widening gap between oil prices and the Australian bowser — and the motorists being left with the bill.*

**Target:** ~3,000-word feature for a major news outlet. General and professional audience.

---

## The Core Thesis

The gap between what crude oil costs and what Australian motorists pay at the pump has nearly tripled — from 43 cents per litre in 2005 to 115 cents in 2025. This widening accelerated sharply post-2020, precisely as EV adoption began its steep climb. The article argues that remaining petrol buyers — disproportionately lower-income, rural, and unable to afford EVs — are effectively subsidising an industry in structural decline and a transition they've been given no affordable way to join.

## Source Material

The article is anchored by three proprietary data visualisations (provided by co-author) showing: (1) crude oil cost vs pump price vs margin over 20 years, overlaid with EV market share; (2) Brent crude USD vs AU petrol price (monthly, Jan 2005–Mar 2026); (3) same data with crude converted to AUD, eliminating currency effects. All three show a post-2020 structural divergence between input cost and retail price.

Key data milestones from the charts: 2005 gap 43¢ → 2008 peak 78¢ → 2016 low 83¢ → 2020 crash 81¢ → 2023: 111¢ → 2025: 115¢. EV share: 0% → 0.7% → 8–12% → 13%.

## Structure (10 sections)

**1. Lede — The 115-cent gap.** Opens with the stark data: crude is cheaper now than in 2013, yet pump prices are near record highs. The margin has ballooned 168%. Sets up the central question.

**2. The divergence.** Walks through the 20-year chart data. The crude-to-pump relationship held for 15 years, then broke post-2020. Even converting crude to AUD (removing currency effects) confirms the structural decoupling. The pump price no longer tracks its input cost.

**3. Anatomy of the margin.** Breaks down the 115¢ stack: fuel excise (~52.6¢, up from ~36¢ in 2005), GST (~16¢), refining margins (volatile, only two domestic refineries remain), wholesale/distribution, and retail margin. Excise and GST growth explain ~30–35¢ of the 72¢ increase. The rest sits in refining, wholesale, and retail — the layers where competitive pressure has weakened.

**4. The volume problem.** EV adoption as economic force, not just environmental aspiration. BEVs + PHEVs at 13.1% of new sales (2025). Pure petrol share fell from 44.7% to 40.1% in one year. AAA projects petrol below 50% of new sales by 2027. Service stations collapsed from ~20,000 (1970) to ~6,300. ACCC explicitly links declining volumes to rising per-litre costs. The transition is structural and accelerating.

**5. The death spiral parallel.** Draws on Simshauser & Nelson's 2012 utility death spiral concept (originally applied to electricity/solar). Same feedback loop: technology shift reduces demand → fixed costs spread over fewer units → per-unit prices rise → alternative becomes more attractive → more customers leave → repeat. Fuel retail is entering this cycle. Retailers can diversify (Ampol: 44% of earnings from convenience), but the underlying trend is irreversible.

**6. Who is left at the pump? — The equity dimension.** EV adoption is led by wealthier, metro areas. Rural EV penetration: 0.39% vs 1.52% in cities. The FBT exemption primarily benefits higher-income earners with access to salary sacrifice. Those still at the bowser are disproportionately lower-income, regional, and locked into vehicle types with no affordable EV alternative. They're absorbing the full 115¢ margin while EV owners contribute nothing to fuel excise.

**7. Industry consolidation.** Ampol's $1.1B bid for 500 EG Australia stations. ACCC flagged 115 competition-risk sites. Four companies supply 88% of Australia's petrol. Independents (now 18→26% market share) are the most effective price constraint, but face structural disadvantage. Majors pivoting to multi-fuel convenience hubs — funded by current fuel margin extraction.

**8. The policy vacuum.** The government is simultaneously promoting EV adoption (NVES from Jan 2025, FBT exemption under review) while having no coherent response to the consequences for fuel pricing and equity. Fuel excise is eroding by design. No federal road-user charge for EVs. ACCC monitors but can't regulate margins. The 2022 excise halving was a temporary fix for a structural problem.

**9. International context.** UK CMA found margins "persistently high" (Dec 2025). US margins structurally lower but also saw a post-2020 step-change. Common thread: COVID gave fuel retailers the cover and commercial imperative to reset pricing structures — and those structures haven't reverted.

**10. What comes next.** The trajectory: more EVs → fewer litres → wider margins → higher cost for those who remain. Policy options are all difficult (universal road-user charge, fuel price regulation, targeted EV subsidies for lower-income households). Closes with the human cost: the tradesman in Dubbo, the single parent in outer Melbourne, the farmer in central Queensland. The 115-cent gap is the price of the EV transition — the question is who should bear it.

- ACCC Dec 2025 report: GIRDs at 17.9 cpl (above 10-year avg); COVID peak: 19.4 cpl when volumes fell 27% YoY
- FCAI: BEV + PHEV = 13.1% of new sales (2025); petrol share 44.7% → 40.1% in one year; PHEV sales +134% YoY
- AAA: petrol below 50% of new sales projected by 2027
- Ampol: 44% earnings from convenience; Lytton refinery margins A$14.90/bbl (Q1 2023)
- Market structure: 4 companies = 88% supply; 2 refineries; ~6,300 stations (down from ~20,000)
- Equity: rural EV 0.39% vs metro 1.52%; FBT exemption benefits higher-income salary sacrifice
- Policy: NVES from 1 Jan 2025 (61% reduction target by 2029); Victoria EV road charge 2.8¢/km; no federal equivalent
- UK CMA: margins "persistently high"; supermarket 9.6 ppl, non-supermarket 11.1 ppl
- Fuel excise: 52.6 cpl (CPI-indexed), up from ~36¢ in 2005

## Tone and Approach

Data-driven investigative feature. The article lets the numbers lead, avoids polemics, and presents the equity argument as an implication of the data rather than an editorial position. Closes with concrete human examples rather than abstract policy prescriptions. The framing draws a parallel to the well-documented utility death spiral, grounding a novel argument in established economic theory.
