# Who Pays for the EV Transition?

## Inside the widening gap between oil prices and the Australian bowser — and the motorists being left with the bill

---

In 2005, when a barrel of crude oil translated to roughly 36 cents per litre in Australian currency, drivers paid 113 cents at the pump. The difference — 43 cents — covered taxes, refining, distribution, and the retailer's cut. It was unremarkable. It was the cost of doing business.

Twenty years later, crude oil sits at 62 cents per litre in Australian dollar terms — cheaper, in real input cost, than it was in 2013 or 2023. Yet the national average pump price has climbed to 177 cents. The gap has ballooned to 115 cents per litre. That is not a rounding error. It is not inflation alone. It is a 168 per cent increase in the spread between what oil costs and what Australian motorists pay, and it demands an explanation.

The answer lies at the intersection of declining fuel volumes, accelerating electric vehicle adoption, an entrenched industry scrambling to protect its margins, and a policy environment that has yet to reckon with the distributional consequences. The data tells a story that no one in Canberra, and few in the industry, is willing to say plainly: the Australians least able to participate in the electric vehicle revolution are the ones financing it — one litre at a time.

---

### The divergence

For most of the past two decades, the relationship between crude oil prices and Australian pump prices followed a recognisable pattern. When Brent crude spiked to nearly US$150 a barrel during the 2008 commodity boom, Australian pump prices surged to 165 cents per litre. When crude collapsed during the COVID-19 demand shock in early 2020, pump prices fell to 99 cents. The lines moved together, separated by a relatively stable margin.

That relationship has broken.

Charts tracking Brent crude converted to Australian dollars against the national average unleaded petrol price show a gap that began widening around 2020 and has since become a chasm. In the second chart — plotting monthly Brent crude in USD against Australian ULP in cents per litre from January 2005 to March 2026 — the two lines, which once moved in near-lockstep, now inhabit different orbits. The pump price line has decoupled upward, hovering between 160 and 240 cents per litre even as crude has retreated from its 2022 peaks.

When the same data is plotted with crude converted to Australian dollars (eliminating the currency effect), the divergence is even more stark. The pump price is no longer tracking its input cost. Something structural has changed.

The milestones tell the story in compressed form. In 2005, the gap was 43 cents. By the 2008 oil price peak, it had widened to 78 cents — explainable by the sheer price level and proportional taxes. By 2016, when crude hit multi-year lows at 27 cents per litre equivalent, the gap was 83 cents: oil had fallen but the pump price didn't follow it all the way down. During the 2020 COVID crash, with crude at just 18 cents per litre equivalent, the gap was 81 cents. And then the trajectory steepened. By 2023, with crude at 75 cents, the gap had reached 111 cents. In 2025, with crude actually falling to 62 cents, the gap widened further still — to 115 cents.

What is in that 115 cents?

---

### Anatomy of the margin

The gap between crude cost and pump price is not a single number controlled by a single actor. It is a stack: federal fuel excise, the goods and services tax, refining margins, wholesale and distribution costs, and the retail margin.

Fuel excise, indexed to the Consumer Price Index twice yearly, currently stands at 52.6 cents per litre — up from roughly 36 cents in 2005. That accounts for roughly 16 to 17 cents of the margin's growth over two decades. The GST, levied at 10 per cent on the total pump price including excise, adds another 16 cents or so at current prices.

But excise and GST together explain perhaps 30 to 35 cents of the 72-cent increase in the gap since 2005. Where is the rest?

Refining margins have been volatile. In 2022, when global supply dislocations hit, refining margins surged — Ampol's Lytton refinery saw margins reach A$14.90 per barrel in early 2023. With only two refineries remaining in Australia (Ampol's in Brisbane and Viva Energy's in Geelong), the country imports more than half its refined fuel, exposing it to international refining margin swings and freight costs that are beyond any domestic policy lever.

Wholesale and distribution costs have risen with insurance, logistics, and compliance requirements. But the figure that has drawn the most regulatory attention is the retail margin itself.

The ACCC's December 2025 petroleum market report put gross indicative retail differences — a measure capturing both operating costs and profit at the retail level — at 17.9 cents per litre, up 1.5 cents from the previous quarter and above the 10-year real terms average. That figure, while it sounds modest, represents just one layer of a multi-layered stack. When the ACCC looked at periods of acute demand decline — the COVID-19 lockdowns of mid-2020, when volumes fell 27 per cent year-on-year — gross retail margins hit a record 19.4 cents per litre. The pattern is unmistakable: when fewer litres flow through the system, margins per litre rise.

---

### The volume problem

This is where the electric vehicle enters the frame — not as an environmental aspiration but as an economic force reshaping the commercial logic of fuel retail.

In 2020, electric and plug-in hybrid vehicles accounted for 0.7 per cent of new car sales in Australia. By 2023, that share had reached somewhere between 8 and 12 per cent. In 2025, battery electric vehicles and plug-in hybrids together claimed 13.1 per cent of the new vehicle market — 156,857 units — with plug-in hybrid sales alone surging 134 per cent year-on-year. The Federal Chamber of Automotive Industries reported that pure petrol vehicle sales fell from 44.7 per cent of the light vehicle market in 2024 to 40.1 per cent in 2025.

These are not incremental shifts. The Australian Automobile Association has projected that new petrol vehicle sales could fall below 50 per cent of the market as soon as 2027.

Every vehicle that switches from petrol to electric power removes demand from a fuel retail network built for a different era. Australia's network of service stations has already contracted from roughly 20,000 sites in 1970 to about 6,300 today. The remaining stations carry fixed costs — leases, staff, equipment, environmental compliance, branding — that do not shrink when fewer litres are sold. Those costs must be recovered from the litres that remain.

The ACCC has stated this dynamic explicitly. Petrol retailing, the regulator noted, is a high-volume, low-margin business with many fixed costs. When sales volumes decline, the cost per unit of petrol increases. Conversely, higher volumes spread fixed costs more thinly, lowering the per-litre burden.

This is not a temporary phenomenon tied to a demand shock that will reverse. The EV transition is structural and accelerating. Each quarter that passes adds tens of thousands of electric and plug-in hybrid vehicles to Australian roads, permanently displacing petrol demand. The fuel industry's response — widening margins to protect per-litre returns as volumes erode — is economically rational for the individual firm. For the system, it is the beginning of a spiral.

---

### A familiar spiral

Energy economists will recognise the pattern. In 2012, AGL economists Paul Simshauser and Tim Nelson described what they called the utility death spiral — a feedback loop in which customers adopt distributed generation or improve efficiency, reducing demand, causing per-unit prices to rise, driving more customers to leave the system, and so on until the business model becomes untenable.

Australia's electricity sector lived through a version of this dynamic as rooftop solar proliferated, eroding demand for grid-supplied power and forcing network costs onto a shrinking base of non-solar customers.

Fuel retail now faces its own version. The mechanism is the same: a technology shift (EVs replacing internal combustion) permanently reduces demand for the incumbent product (petrol). Fixed infrastructure costs must be spread across fewer units. Per-unit prices rise. The rising price makes the alternative technology more attractive. More customers switch. The cycle reinforces itself.

There are differences, of course. Fuel retailers can diversify into convenience retail (and have — Ampol derives 44 per cent of earnings from convenience operations, not fuel). They can install EV charging infrastructure at existing sites. They can pursue consolidation to capture economies of scale. But none of these strategies reverses the underlying trend: fewer litres sold each year, at higher margins, to a diminishing pool of petrol buyers.

---

### Who is left at the pump?

This is where the margin data stops being an abstract chart and becomes a question of equity.

Electric vehicle adoption in Australia is not evenly distributed. Research consistently shows that EV uptake is led by wealthier areas and metropolitan centres, where charging infrastructure is dense and salary sacrifice arrangements — the primary mechanism through which the federal Fringe Benefits Tax exemption for EVs operates — are accessible. Rural EV penetration lags metropolitan rates by a factor of four to five, sitting at roughly 0.39 per cent compared to 1.52 per cent in cities.

The Australians still filling up at the bowser in 2025 and 2026 are, disproportionately, those who cannot afford a new electric vehicle, do not have access to workplace salary sacrifice schemes, live in areas with limited charging infrastructure, or drive vehicle types (utes, large SUVs, towing vehicles) for which affordable electric alternatives do not yet exist. Many are in regional and rural communities. Many are lower-income households for whom fuel is a non-discretionary expense.

These motorists are now absorbing the full weight of a 115-cent margin — nearly three times what their counterparts paid twenty years ago in real terms — while the wealthier, urban early adopters of EVs pay nothing at all into the fuel excise system that funds road infrastructure.

The distributional calculus is uncomfortable. The FBT exemption, which has been one of the most effective policy levers for EV adoption, disproportionately benefits higher-income earners. The fuel excise system, which raises tens of billions annually for road and transport infrastructure, collects nothing from EV owners. Victoria introduced a road-user charge for zero-emission vehicles at 2.8 cents per kilometre, but the measure remains politically contentious and has not been replicated federally.

The result is a system in which the costs of transition are borne regressively. Those with the means to switch capture the savings of cheap electricity and tax exemptions. Those without the means to switch pay escalating fuel costs that include not only the legitimate costs of refining and distribution but also the margin inflation driven by the very transition they cannot join.

---

### An industry consolidating around decline

The fuel retail industry's response to this structural shift has been consolidation. In 2026, Ampol mounted a $1.1 billion bid to acquire 500 EG Australia service stations — a move that would dramatically concentrate the retail network. The ACCC identified 115 sites where the acquisition could reduce competition, underscoring the tension between industry rationalisation and consumer protection.

Four companies — Ampol, BP, ExxonMobil, and Viva Energy — collectively supply 88 per cent of Australia's petrol. With only two domestic refineries operating and more than half of refined fuel imported, the supply chain offers limited competitive pressure points.

Independent retailers, whose market share has grown from 18 per cent to 26 per cent over the past six years, provide the most effective competitive constraint on retail pricing. The ACCC has found that petrol prices are generally cheaper in cities with more independent operators. But independents face their own pressures — thin margins, volatile wholesale costs, and infrastructure investment requirements that favour larger players.

Meanwhile, the majors are hedging. Ampol and Viva Energy have both signalled strategies to transform service stations into multi-fuel convenience hubs, incorporating EV charging alongside traditional fuel sales. The logic is pragmatic: the site has the real estate, the traffic flow, and the retail infrastructure. But the transition requires capital investment at a time when the core fuel business is being asked to generate the returns to fund it — creating another layer of upward pressure on per-litre margins.

---

### The policy vacuum

The Australian government's position on fuel pricing and the EV transition contains a fundamental tension that no current policy framework resolves.

On one hand, Canberra is actively promoting EV adoption. The New Vehicle Efficiency Standard, which commenced on 1 January 2025, requires vehicle suppliers to meet progressively tighter fleet-wide CO2 targets — a 61 per cent reduction for passenger vehicles by 2029. The FBT exemption for electric vehicles, introduced in 2022 and currently under review by Treasurer Jim Chalmers, has been a major driver of fleet and salary-sacrifice EV uptake. Public submissions on the exemption's future closed in February 2026, with forecasts suggesting its removal could reduce EV sales by nearly 30,000 units annually.

On the other hand, the government has no coherent policy response to the consequences of that promotion on fuel pricing and fuel equity. Fuel excise remains the primary funding mechanism for road infrastructure, yet the tax base is eroding by design. No federal road-user charge for EVs has been implemented. The ACCC monitors fuel prices but has no mandate to regulate margins. And while the temporary halving of fuel excise in 2022 demonstrated that Canberra can intervene when pump prices become politically unbearable, the structural widening of the crude-to-pump margin is not a problem that a temporary excise cut can fix.

The policy gap is not merely one of taxation. It extends to the distributional question that the screenshots at the top of this analysis make visible. If the margin between crude and pump continues to widen — as the structural dynamics of declining volume and fixed-cost recovery suggest it will — the cost of Australia's EV transition will be borne disproportionately by those who cannot participate in it. That is not a market failure in the traditional sense. It is a policy choice expressed through inaction.

### An international pattern

Australia is not alone in this dynamic, but the scale of its margin widening is notable.

In the United Kingdom, the Competition and Markets Authority found in December 2025 that fuel retail margins remain "persistently high" and cannot be explained by operating costs alone. UK supermarket margins sat at 9.6 pence per litre, with non-supermarket margins at 11.1 pence — both well above pre-2020 levels. UK fuel duty, at 52.95 pence per litre, is nearly identical to Australia's excise rate.

In the United States, retail margins averaged 40 cents per US gallon (approximately 14 Australian cents per litre) in December 2025, with net profit after operating expenses running at 10 to 15 cents per gallon. American margins are structurally lower, reflecting domestic oil production, a more fragmented retail market, and vastly lower fuel taxes.

The common thread across all three markets is the post-2020 step-change in margins. The COVID demand shock appears to have provided fuel retailers with both the cover and the commercial imperative to reset pricing structures — and those structures have not reverted even as demand partially recovered. In Australia, where EV adoption is accelerating faster than in the US (though behind Northern Europe), the volume-driven margin pressure adds a structural overlay to what was initially a cyclical adjustment.

---

### What comes next

The trajectory embedded in the data is not encouraging for anyone hoping the margin will compress. If EV and plug-in hybrid sales continue on their current path — reaching 15 per cent or more of new sales in 2026, with total electrified vehicles (including conventional hybrids) approaching 30 per cent — each year will remove more petrol demand from the system. Each year, fixed costs will be spread across fewer litres. Each year, the commercial pressure to maintain or widen per-litre margins will intensify.

The policy responses available are neither simple nor popular. A universal road-user charge for all vehicles — not just EVs — would broaden the tax base but face fierce political resistance. Targeted fuel price regulation would distort market signals and likely accelerate station closures in marginal areas. Direct subsidies for lower-income households to transition to EVs or hybrids would address the equity dimension but require substantial fiscal commitment.

What cannot continue is the current pretence that the EV transition is cost-free for those left behind. The charts show a margin that has nearly tripled in two decades. The bottom panel of the first chart shows an EV adoption curve that is still in its early exponential phase. The intersection of these two trends — rising per-litre costs and falling per-litre volumes — is not a future scenario. It is the present.

The 115-cent gap between crude and bowser is a number with a human cost. It is paid by the tradesman filling a diesel ute in Dubbo, the single parent commuting from the outer suburbs of Melbourne, the farmer in central Queensland who has no public transport alternative. They are paying more per litre not because oil is expensive — it isn't — but because the economics of fuel retail are being reshaped by a transition they have been given no affordable way to join.

That gap is the price of the EV transition. The question the data poses, and that policy must answer, is who should bear it.
